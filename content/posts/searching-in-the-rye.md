*The Catcher in the Rye* sets itself up in opposition to “The goddam movies,” as Holden Caulfield says. Now, I am a fan of the movies. I grew up on a diet of film; it is, after all, our generation’s first language. And yet, I can see why Salinger would want to set himself up in opposition to this image-driven world.

Since the rise of ISIL last year, there have been countless killings, but none so horrifying as the dozen beheadings, each filmed, shared online, and viewed millions of times. Or take the atrocities in Paris a few weeks ago—within one day, the BBC had shown us the carnage at a rock concert and footage of a woman clinging to a windowsill, desperately trying to escape her killers.

Fetishized images of carnage frame the debate, triggering our most instinctual fight-or-flight responses. Of course, there is nothing new in this morbid fascination with death and brutality. As long as judicial violence has existed, spectators have filed into coliseums to watch. The difference today is that such images are readily available in our technosociety. We all have anonymous front-row seats, and these images, more powerful than ever, shape our reality.

### Hyper-Reality and Illusion

Snapshots like these appear to offer us the reality of the situation—pictures and films of what’s “really happening” stream into our homes 24 hours a day. And yet, this hyper-reality, uncontextualized, plays into false narratives that keep us sedated in illusions. We willingly orient ourselves in favor or against pre-defined narratives of convenience, a narrative of good versus evil. When we strip away these illusions, the weight of our isolated aloneness becomes painfully apparent.

This search for genuine human connection—resisting the trite, the cliché, the phony—is what *The Catcher in the Rye* is all about. Not a great deal happens in the book. Holden Caulfield, a 17-year-old boy, is expelled from school and drifts through New York for a few days, trying to connect with someone. He fails repeatedly. The cruel and phony adult world doesn’t just treat people like Holden poorly—it kills them (metaphorically speaking).

### Holden Caulfield’s Connection

Holden’s journey towards integrity in the adult world is the book’s destination, though he doesn’t fully arrive. Yet, he achieves something profound by connecting—not with any character, but with us. We empathize with Holden. We see his pain and loneliness. In this way, Holden makes a true and meaningful human connection.

Consider the passage where Holden enters a phone booth to call someone. He spends ages thinking about whom to call and concludes there’s no one. It’s a moment steeped in isolation. Yet the Holden recounting this pain is the one connecting with us.

Genuine human connection is incredibly difficult. We so often fall into trite speech patterns or meaningless pleasantries. To truly hear another person is a lifelong endeavor. Hence the value of silence, of being still in one another’s company.

### J.D. Salinger and Human Connection

J.D. Salinger himself struggled deeply with human connection. He witnessed firsthand the atrocities of WWII—fighting on D-Day, at the Battle of the Bulge, and being one of the first to enter Nazi extermination camps. Unlike many literary contemporaries who wrote about war, Salinger wrote about a boy trying to connect with others.

Ultimately, Salinger retreated from the limelight, living out the rest of his life in a Thoreauvian cabin until his death in 2010. It is rumored that during this period of isolation, he wrote several unpublished books, which his estate may release in the coming years.

### The Catcher in the Rye’s Legacy

*The Catcher in the Rye* should open our eyes to the meaningless noise that occupies so much of our time and help us put things into perspective. More importantly, it should illuminate the quiet desperation endured by so many and inspire us to reach out before it’s too late—especially as we approach Christmas.

The book also has a sinister reputation. Some, like Mark David Chapman, have empathized with Holden’s plight but failed to make meaningful connections, lashing out in their desperation. Chapman, who murdered John Lennon in 1980, left a copy of *The Catcher in the Rye* at the crime scene with the note: “This is my statement.”

Holden’s desire to be “the catcher in the rye” encapsulates the book’s spirit:

> “Anyway, I keep picturing all these little kids playing some game in this big field of rye and all. Thousands of little kids, and nobody’s around—nobody big, I mean—except me. And I’m standing on the edge of some crazy cliff. What I have to do, I have to catch everybody if they start to go over the cliff—I mean if they’re running and they don’t look where they’re going, I have to come out from somewhere and catch them. That’s all I do all day. I’d just be the catcher in the rye and all. I know it’s crazy, but that’s the only thing I’d really like to be.”
