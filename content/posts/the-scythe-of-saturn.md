“Unfortunately, I am a completely impractical person, caught up in endless trains of thought. All of us are fantasists, ill-equipped for life, the children as much as myself. It seems to me sometimes that we never get used to being on this earth and life is just one great, ongoing, incomprehensible blunder.” These words from W. G. Sebald, in his most notable book *The Rings of Saturn*, encapsulate much of the melancholy and existential contemplation found within its pages.

The title, *The Rings of Saturn*, might evoke images of science fiction—a starship whizzing past a Saturn-like planet. However, the title is steeped in mythology. The Romans deified the planets, seeing them as representations of divine forces: Mars as the God of War, Mercury as the God of commerce, and Saturn as the God of Time, often depicted as an elderly figure with a scythe and hourglass. Sebald likens Saturn’s scythe to the earth’s slow turn to darkness, writing, “The shadow of the night is drawn like a black veil across the earth... one might, in following the setting sun, see on our globe nothing but prone bodies, row upon row, as if leveled by the scythe of Saturn—an endless graveyard for a humanity struck by falling sickness.”

### Exploring *The Rings of Saturn*

Sebald’s *The Rings of Saturn* defies categorization. It is a travelogue, a memoir, and a work of fiction. It begins: 

> “In August 1992, when the dog days were drawing to an end, I set off to walk the county of Suffolk in the hope of dispelling the emptiness that takes hold of me whenever I have completed a long stint of work, and in fact my hope is released, up to a point, for I have seldom felt so carefree as I did then…”

Yet, what starts as a walking tour of Suffolk spirals into meditations on time, memory, destruction, and identity. Sebald’s journey begins at Lowestoft and ends at Bungay, but each place he visits becomes a springboard for digressions and reflections. His writing mirrors the concentric rings of Saturn—shattered fragments orbiting a central theme of decay and impermanence.

Sebald, a German-born writer who moved to England in 1967, grappled with the weight of history, particularly the atrocities of WWII. His thoughts on destruction and memory permeate the book, woven into Suffolk’s natural beauty. The collapse of coastal cliffs, the dwindling fishing industry, and historical atrocities merge into a broader narrative of loss.

### The Photographic Shards

Throughout *The Rings of Saturn*, Sebald includes dark, grainy photographs. These serve as visual shards—fragments that echo the themes of memory and destruction. One striking photograph depicts a sea of corpses amidst a tranquil forest, an image of Bergen-Belsen concentration camp’s liberation. The forest canopy obscures the horizon, severing the viewer from context. 

Similarly, a photograph of fishermen standing among a glut of herring in Lowestoft mirrors the earlier image. The fish, like the corpses, become a monument to destruction—a fleeting abundance that rots and disappears. Sebald notes the phosphorescent glow of herring after death, drawing a parallel to memory: “Whenever a shift in our spiritual life occurs and fragments such as these surface, we believe we can remember. But in reality of course, memory fails us.”

### Walking as a Romantic Tradition

Sebald’s journey aligns with the British Romantic tradition of walking as a process of recovery. Writers like Wordsworth and Coleridge saw nature as a refuge from modernity’s hyper-rationalism. However, for Sebald, walking does not lead to healing. Instead, it reveals the fragmentary and transient nature of existence. He writes about the idyllic sense of home—an imagined past of safety and security—but warns that inhabiting this ideal would be its own kind of oppressive hell. 

Sebald’s subtitle, *An English Pilgrimage*, evokes a journey of spiritual significance. Yet, his pilgrimage offers no resolution or redemption. Instead, he accepts the loss of meaning and finds solace in the meandering, in moving through concentric circles of thought and place.

### Conclusion

Sebald’s *The Rings of Saturn* invites us to confront the fragmentary nature of memory and the inevitability of decay. It challenges the search for meaning, suggesting instead that life’s scattered shards—like the rings of Saturn—are enough. Through his melancholic reflections, Sebald encourages us to embrace the meandering journey, to find beauty in the transitory and the incomplete.

Let us walk, not in search of answers, but in recognition of the fragments that make up our lives.
