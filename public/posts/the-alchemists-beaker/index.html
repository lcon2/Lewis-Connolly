The Alchemist’s Beaker

February 11, 2019

Over the last two centuries, many in the West have felt a spiritual void within their own Western Christian heritage. To supplement that perceived lack, they have looked to the East. The allure of Oriental spirituality has captured the imaginations of many. We can think of the Eastern influence on the American Transcendentalists, the 1960s countercultural movement, Schopenhauer’s impact on European intellectual thought, the presence of meditation and yoga in everyday culture, or the various iterations of New Age spirituality with an Eastern flavor. There is no doubt that Eastern mysticism has profoundly influenced the West. Within our own Unitarian movement, many have historically identified with Eastern spirituality in one way or another.

Yet, many spiritual leaders have cautioned against Westerners over-identifying with Eastern religious movements. The Dalai Lama once said, “I always tell my Western friends that it is best to keep your own traditions. Changing religions is not easy and sometimes causes confusion. You must value your tradition and honor your own religion.” Alan Watts advised, “Western people need to exercise a great deal of discrimination and caution in adopting Eastern disciplines and ways of life.” Even Ram Dass remarked, “I have always said that often the religion you were born with becomes more important to you as you see the universality of truth.”

Rooted in the West

The contention is this: as Westerners, we are rooted in the landscape of ideas as they find expression in the West. We have Western psyches; we think, respond, and resonate most naturally with philosophies and religions with roots in the West. Christianity, though originating in the Middle East, has (for the most part) developed a distinctly Western flavor. Christian denominations in the West explore their faith through Western questions and biases, framed by Western preoccupations.

I suspect that the frequent turn to Eastern spirituality within our Unitarian movement is, in part, a psychological compensation. Our Unitarian Christian heritage is among the most Western expressions of Christianity, emanating as it does from a European Enlightenment and rationalistic base. To sate our natural thirst for a deeper, more mystical and holistic spirituality, we might resist the call of the East and instead look to the mystery traditions within our own Western context—specifically, Western Alchemy.

The Symbolism of Alchemy

Alchemy, at first glance, might appear inaccessible, filled with obscure symbols and terminology. It conjures images of reclusive old men in laboratories on fool’s errands, trying to turn lead into gold. At best, it is seen as the precursor to modern chemistry. But alchemy is not just about manipulating matter; it is a metaphor for transforming the human soul.

In this metaphor, we are the beaker—our bodies, our whole selves. Within the beaker are various components: our mediating self, interests, beliefs, sensations, childhood memories, impressions, and dreams. Much of this content is unconscious. Chemistry today is precise, but alchemy embraces the unknown, acknowledging the imperfections and unexamined elements within us.

Change occurs when the beaker is subjected to the white-hot flame and given time to ferment. This flame might be an intentional process of self-exploration, or it might come from external forces: conflict, loss, social pressure, or being forced outside our comfort zone.

The Role of the Alchemist

This transformative process can be facilitated by a skilled practitioner—a spiritual leader, therapist, or insightful friend—who introduces new elements to the beaker. These elements might be confrontational, illuminating fears or shadows, but ultimately spark transformation. The beaker can symbolize an individual, a relationship, a family, a community, a nation, or even the world.

As a preacher, I see my role as an alchemist. I listen, intuit, and observe the alchemical process within our church community. With careful consideration, I add new elements to the church beaker, aiming to catalyze positive transformation. This process is challenging but can be deeply nourishing, offering spiritual growth for those willing to engage.

The Stages of Transformation

The alchemist speaks of the first emanation—a black, putrid froth. These are the throes of melancholy, the dark night of the soul. In time, the darkness clears, and a new day breaks. The opus magnum is finished, and from the chaos emerges integration, wholeness, and completeness. This is the chemical wedding—the philosopher’s stone.

May we embrace the alchemical process in our own lives, transforming through fire into something greater.

Amen.
