Pale Fire: Lunar Light

July 23, 2018

This week, a few of us met in the upper room to discuss how we understand the person of Jesus. Our conversation circled back to the issue of interpretation a few times. How are we to interpret his parables? How are we to interpret his miracles? How are we to interpret his death upon the cross? The point was made that Christian denominations often present Jesus in ways that serve their own interests or affirm their pre-held assumptions. We see what we want to see.

It’s one of the oddities of reading the Bible: the conservative uncovers a conservative image of Jesus, and the liberal uncovers a liberal image of Jesus. And yet they’re reading the same text. This raises profound questions about interpretation.

Creedal vs. Non-Creedal Interpretations

Creedal denominations often present particular interpretations of Jesus as definitive. The goal is not so much understanding as it is assent. The answer is preordained; the believer’s task is to accept it. Failure to understand may be seen as a deficiency of faith or insight, and so, many give their assent without pause.

Non-creedal traditions, however, amplify the individual’s role in understanding, making the art of interpretation critical. In these contexts, agreement is never a given—it depends on whether the argument is convincing. Yet, this approach raises its own issues. A compelling argument may not necessarily correlate with truth, and communities governed by persuasion may risk being led astray.

Nabokov’s Pale Fire and the Problem of Interpretation

Vladimir Nabokov’s postmodern novel Pale Fire sheds light on the complexities of interpretation. The novel comprises a 999-line poem by the fictional poet John Shade, followed by a commentary by Shade’s self-proclaimed academic colleague, Charles Kinbote.

Shade’s poem wrestles with personal grief, particularly the suicide of his daughter, exploring themes of loss, the human condition, and the possibility of an afterlife. However, Kinbote’s commentary skews the interpretation entirely. Obsessed with himself, Kinbote attempts to frame the poem as a cryptic narrative about his own life. His commentary grows increasingly delusional, revealing him as a narcissist who manipulates the poem to fit his agenda.

Here is an excerpt from Shade’s poem:

I was an infant when my parents died.
They both were ornithologists. I’ve tried
So often to evoke them that today
I have a thousand parents. Sadly they
Dissolve in their own virtues and recede,
But certain words, chance words I hear or read,
Such as “bad heart” always to him refer,
And “cancer of the pancreas” to her.

This passage reflects Shade’s grief and his imaginative attempts to reconstruct his parents, who died when he was an infant. His reflections are both deeply personal and tinged with philosophical questions about loss and memory.

Kinbote’s Commentary

Kinbote’s commentary on the poem is absurdly self-referential. For example, he claims a marginal note in the poem connects to a Zemblan quatrain he once quoted to Shade:

“The wise at nightfall praise the day,
The wife when she has passed away,
The ice when it is crossed, the bride
When tumbled and the horse when tried.”

This analysis bears no relevance to Shade’s poem and serves only to bolster Kinbote’s delusions that the poem is about him. By the end, it becomes clear that Kinbote is entirely unreliable, a critique of literary criticism itself and its propensity to impose subjective meanings.

Lunar Light and the Nature of Truth

The title Pale Fire references a line from Shakespeare’s Timon of Athens: “The moon’s an arrant thief, and her pale fire she snatches from the sun.” The moon’s light, derived from the sun, serves as a metaphor for interpretation. It suggests that interpretations are but reflections of the truth, yet these reflections can mislead or obscure the original.

Richard Rorty’s introduction to the novel adds an intriguing layer. He likens Kinbote’s world to Prospero’s island in The Tempest—a world where interpretation reigns supreme, untethered from an external truth. In Pale Fire, there is no ultimate reality behind the poem, only Kinbote’s obsessive lens.

Closing Thoughts

Interpretation is an inescapable part of human understanding, whether in literature, theology, or life itself. While interpretations may not always capture the sun—truth itself—they remain our means of navigating the night. To quote Nabokov, “The moon’s an arrant thief”, but she is also the light by which we see.

Amen.
