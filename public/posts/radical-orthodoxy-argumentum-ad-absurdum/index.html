Radical Orthodoxy: argumentum ad absurdum

November 22, 2015

It is no secret that I found my time training for the priesthood at Westcott House grating. There were a number of reasons for this, but the underlying issue was the degree to which my own theological opinions jarred with the ethos of the College. Now, three years on, I find myself in the incredibly weird situation of living once again in an Anglican training college—this time in Oxford, while my wife trains for the priesthood. As a Unitarian, my perspective on these Anglican training establishments must be quite unique.

Over the last couple of months, it seems like the Unitarian movement as a whole has been thinking more deeply about the nature of its own identity, particularly in light of the Vision for the Future publication earlier this year. As I survey the landscape of British Unitarianism today, and consider where it has come over the last century, I cannot help but compare it to the Anglo-Catholic tradition from which I came. The Church of England is a broad church, an uneasy alliance of divergent parties maintained by a veil of ignorance.

I was initially drawn to Anglicanism through two writers, John Robinson and Don Cupitt, for whom I have a great deal of love and admiration. Both trained for the priesthood at Westcott House. Throughout the latter part of the 20th century, they sought to respond to the demands of the modern world. They rejected the idea of a God “up there” or “out there,” argued for moving away from the magical or supernatural elements of faith, and sought to reimagine traditional Christianity. But their optimistic, radical vision of the church was never realized.

The Rise of Radical Orthodoxy

In today’s “modern Catholic” training colleges, figures like Robinson and Cupitt are largely ignored. The dominant school of thought now is John Milbank’s Radical Orthodoxy. This morning, I want to explore what Radical Orthodoxy is, where it comes from, and what its weaknesses might teach us about shifting trends within our own movement.

Radical Orthodoxy began in the 1990s with a series of books by John Milbank. Its opening premise presents a stark binary: religion or the secular. Milbank claims that secularism leads only to nihilism and that all disciplines underpinned by secular bias—including science, ethics, and politics—must be dismissed and reframed through a theological lens. In doing so, Radical Orthodoxy isolates itself, dismissing critical input from other disciplines.

From there, Radical Orthodoxy seeks to retrieve a historical version of Christianity, specifically Thomas Aquinas’ 13th-century theology, which it lifts out of its context and drops into the present day. The approach sounds absurd—and indeed, it is. The binary framing of secular versus religious is overly simplistic, ignoring the ways secular society is infused with religious expression. Similarly, no society is purely religious or purely secular.

Critique of Postmodernity

Radical Orthodoxy critiques postmodernity, arguing that its relativism robs churches of the confidence to proclaim truth. Ironically, Radical Orthodoxy uses the tools of postmodernity to construct its own framework: a recapitulated Christianity stripped of liberalism’s concerns.

This is problematic. To be a faithful Christian, one must acknowledge Christianity’s developing tradition—from the story of Israel, to the early Church, to Aquinas, the Reformation, biblical criticism, liberal theology, and liberation theology. To excise parts of this tradition is to deny its continuity. Faithfulness requires an authentic response to what we have inherited—not wishing parts of it away.

The Problems of Community

Radical Orthodoxy emphasizes Aristotelian ideas of community formation, drawing heavily from Aquinas. However, its vision of community is narrow and conservative, romanticizing a premodern past. The focus on “formation” within theological institutions echoes this medieval nostalgia, complete with monastic rituals and rigid boundaries.

Defining community narrowly is highly problematic. The impulse to reject such boundaries and throw the windows open is understandable but also flawed. Community must orient itself around a shared narrative, but it need not do so on authoritarian terms. As was explored last week, it is possible to explicitly make room for other religions and philosophical perspectives without betraying our Christian heritage.

Implications for Unitarianism

What lessons can we draw for our own movement? We must resist the temptation to deny aspects of our heritage. As a community, we must articulate our shared ideals while holding fast to our radical inclusivity. We must remain open to questions and criticisms from culture and society, avoiding the trap of retreating into a ghetto of liberal elitism.

Let us be:

A circle of healing.
A circle of friends.
Someplace where we can be free.

Amen.
